Case Study: Rethinking the Dropdown Menu

Sarah Ridley ● Brown University ● February 2025

In this case study, I examined one UI component and considered alternative designs that could increase the component's accesibility, potentially at the risk of learnability, memorability, or efficiency. The UI component I chose is the dropdown component. I explored the accessibility of this component across devices and across modes of interacting with the device. In the sections below, I walk through the process I took in approaching this accessible redesign.

I: Input

This case study focuses on the dropdown menu UI component. I examined three different applications with dropdown menus, including the EdStem website, the Appple Calendar web/mobile application, and the Starbucks website/mobile app. For context, EdStem is a message board platform that facilitates student, TA, and professor interactions, that is commonly used at Brown. I first explored how different modes of input, including the mouse/touchpad, keyboard, and touch (on mobile) enabled different interactions with the dropdown menus in these three applications.

EdStem Web App

Mouse/ Touchpad
  • The top screenshots show EdStem's dropdown menu for filtering discussion posts on the desktop website. When I hover over the filter button with my mouse, the button turns from gray to blue. Once I click on the button, the dropdown menu appears below the filter button. If I move my mouse away, the dropdown menu stays visible, but when I click anywhere other than the dropdown menu, it disappears.
  • If instead I hover over an option while the dropdown menu is open, the option turns blue. Then, if I click one of the options, the menu disappears and updates the filter. However, with my mouse, I cannot select the text in any of the options, nor copy and paste this text elsewhere.
  • One learnable aspect of using the dropdown with my mouse is that the menu button has a down arrow on it. This is learnable because for first-time users, the down arrow is a good affordance to try clicking to cause options to appear below. Something efficient is that the order of the dropdown options never changes, so users can grow a muscle-memory about where to click if they frequently need to filter messages.
  • Considering mouse interactions, one feature that stood out about EdStem's filter dropdown menu was that the dropdown button turns blue on hover, whereas neither Apple Calendar or Starbucks components change color on hover. This makes it easier for a mouse user to see that they are in range to click the dropdown menu button they want to press, in case they have difficulty discerning which components are buttons.
Keyboard
  • To use the dropdown with the keyboard only, I had to press the tab key until the filter dropdown button was selected. Once selected, I then had to press the return key, which opened the dropdown menu. Then, I could use the up and down arrows to change which option was highlighted. When I pressed return, the highlighted option was inputted.
  • With my keyboard, I found that I can't select the text in the dropdown menu. I tried to use the Command + A shortcut, but it selected all the other text on the page except the text in the dropdown menu. Something that I could do with my keyboard was close the dropdown menu without selecting anything with the escape key.
  • I found it memorable and efficient to use the up and down arrows to navigate once the dropdown is open. However, this impedes learnability, since users need to use the tab key to focus on the dropdown button, but then the up and down keys inside the dropdown. To me, this would me easier to learn if users always used the tab key to switch between components and to switch between different options.
  • Compared to Apple Calendar and Starbucks, I found that EdStem did better in terms of accessibility. As described below, both Apple Calendar and Starbucks were more difficult to use with a keyboard, which is often how people with physical disabilities interact with websites.
Touch (Mobile)
  • The bottom screenshots above show my interactions with the filter dropdown menu on the EdStem website from a mobile device. On my phone, the dropdown menu comes up from the bottom of my screen, and covers the entire screen. From there, I can click on of the filters, which closes the dropdown, or click "Cancel", which closes the dropdown without filtering.
  • As with the mouse and keyboard, I cannot highlight the text in the dropdown on my phone. When I try to press and hold on the text, nothing happens. However, one functionality this component does have is scrolling. If there are too many filter options to fit the screen height, I can scroll to see more options below.
  • Something efficient about this dropdown is that experienced users can memorize the positions of two quick taps if they filter posts on EdStem frequently. However, it is not learnable that the open dropdown menu fills the entire screen. This means that new users who accidentally tap the dropdown button will have no idea what they accidentally pressed since the screen is now filled.
  • From a different perspective, this fullscreen dropdown makes the EdStem mobile dropdown more accessible in some ways than the Apple Calendar or Starbucks. Filling the entire screen does make each option bigger, which is more accessible to people who struggle with fine motor controls.

Calendar Mac App/Mobile App

Mouse/ Touchpad
  • The left screenshots show Apple Calendar's dropdown menu for choosing an event's calendar on the desktop web application. When I hover with my mouse over the button that opens the dropdown, it does not change. Once I click on the button, the dropdown menu appears over the button. If I move my mouse on the screen, the dropdown menu stays visible, but when I click anywhere other than the menu, it disappears.
  • While the dropdown menu is visible, if I hover over the options, each option turns pink. This pink is a Mac system setting that users customize. Then, if I click one of the options in the dropdown menu, the menu disappears and updates the selected calendar. With my mouse, I cannot select the text in any of the options in the dropdown menu nor copy and paste this text to somewhere else.
  • One gap in learnability is that the dropdown menu has an up arrow and a down arrow on the button. I don't think this is learnable because for a dropdown, I would expect just a down arrow. There is also no text on this button that says anything about choosing a calendar. However, this is efficient from a space perspective, and as users get experienced with Apple Calendar, they already know what the button does.
  • Compared to EdStem and Starbucks, something accesible about Apple Calendar dropdown menu is that the text color changes when I hover on one of the dropdown option. This is because the background color changes to pink, so the text changes to white to ensure contrast for those with visual impairments.
Keyboard
  • Using only the keyboard to navigate this dropdown menu on Apple Calendar was virtually impossible. With a calendar event open, as I tabbed through the options on the editing window, the dropdown menu button was never focused. As I later learned, it only tabbed into focus with VoiceOver activated. After using my mouse to open the dropdown, I was able to use the up and down keys to switch between options.
  • Like the EdStem dropdown, I couldn't select the text in the dropdown menu. When I pressed Command + A with the dropdown open, nothing happened. When I pressed Command C, it closed the dropdown menu. Then, I pasted in a text file to see if it had copied anything, and it pastd all the information about the calendar event except the dropdown calendar options.
  • I found it frustrating that I couldn't navigate into the calendar dropdown with my keyboard only. This component isn't learnable, memorable, nor efficient if it can't be used at all.
  • This component not being navigable with the keyboard is also inherently inaccessible. This is a mismatch, since the dropdown can only be used with a mouse or VoiceOver. Those who rely on their keyboard due to physical impairments therefore likely struggle to change the calendar that their events are assigned to.
Touch (Mobile)
  • The right screenshots above show my interactions with the calendar dropdown menu on the mobile Apple Calendar app. On my phone, the dropdown menu pulls up and covers the right half of my screen. I can scroll when the options overflow, click to the left to leave the dropdown, or select an options.
  • As on desktop, I cannot highlight the text in the dropdown on my phone. When I try to press and hold on the text, nothing happens, and when I release, the dropdown menu select whichever calendar whose text I was trying to highlight. Similarly, this component does not support multiple selections, so I can only assign an event to one calendar.
  • On mobile, the dropdown is more learnable than desktop in the sense that on mobile, the name of the current calendar is on the dropdown menu button. This makes it more learnable and memorable because someone using the app for the first time, or the first time in a while, will more quickly understand that the dropdown will let them select a different calendar for this event.
  • Compared to EdStem and Starbucks, something that makes the Apple Calendars mobile dropdown less accessible is that does not fill the screen by default. This makes it easier to accidentally click out of the Apple Calendars dropdown. For the EdStem and Starbucks mobile dropdowns, you can only close them (without making a selection) with an explicit "Cancel" or "X" button.

Starbucks Web App/Mobile App

Mouse/ Touchpad
  • The left screenshots show my interactions with the dropdown for choosing a milk when ordering on Starbucks' website. When I hover with my mouse over the button that opens the dropdown, the button does not change. When I click it, it turns green and the dropdown menu opens over it. If I move my mouse on the screen, the menu stays visible, but when I click anywhere other than the dropdown menu, it closes.
  • With the dropdown menu open, if I hover over the milk options, each option turns pink. Like with the calendar app, this pink is a customizable Mac system setting. If I click one of the options in the dropdown menu, the menu disappears and updates the selected milk. This menu appears to use Apple's built-in dropdown, so naturally it functions very similarly to the Apple Calendar dropdowns.
  • The text above the dropdown menu says "Milk", and there is a down arrow on the button, which both enhance learnabilty. Specifically, the arrow is a clear affordance for where to click. Something that could enhance efficiency would be adding a "Favorites" section at the top of the dropdown where they could save their preferred milk for easy clicking, since repeat customers often have strong milk preferences.
  • Regarding accessibility, unlike EdStem, hovering over Starbucks' dropdown menu button does not change its color. Changing colors on hover would enhance accessibility, since the color signals to mouse users with motor control impairment that they are in the right area to click.
Keyboard
  • With my keyboard, I used the tab key to focus on the dropdown button, but when I pressed the return key, the menu did not open. I later learned that with VoiceOver on, I could use the keys Control + Option + Space to open the menu. However, this combination does not work when VoiceOver is off. When I use my mouse to open the dropdown, I could use the up and down keys to navigate the options.
  • Like the EdStem and Apple Calendar dropdowns, I couldn't select the text in the dropdown. I tried Command + A, but this closed the dropdown menu and highlighted all the other text on the page. Also, nothing happened when I tried to drag and drop the text from the dropdown elsewhere, except for closing the dropdown.
  • Similar to Apple Calendar, I was disappointed that I failed to navigate into the milk dropdown with my keyboard. Again, this impedes the learnability, memorability, and efficiency of this feature.
  • This lack of keyboard navigability is also an accessibility flaw, given that users with physical disabilities are the most likley to use a keyboard to navigate their screen. These dropdowns that are completely unusable without a mouse or VoiceOver create a mismatch that increases the difficulty of using this site.
Touch (Mobile)
  • The right screenshots above show my interactions with the calendar dropdown menu on the mobile Starbucks app. On my phone, the dropdown menu comes up from the bottom and covers almost the entire screen. I can scroll when the options overflow, click to the X to leave the dropdown, or select a milk option.
  • An interesting functionality of dropdown is that I can minimize the dropdown menu by dragging the top halfway down my screen. Now, the menu only takes up the bottom half, as shown in my screenshots. Like the other mobile applications, I cannot highlight the text in the dropdown.
  • I appreciate that the Starbucks website and mobile app have very similar dropdown designs. This makes the platforms more efficient for users who switch between the website and the mobile app. This also makes one more learnable for those who have already used the other.
  • Compared to the EdStem and Apple Calendar, an accessibility flaw with the Starbucks' mobile dropdown is the similarity in motions to scroll and exit. While there is a small "X" button, the more intuitive option for many users is to swipe down to close the menu. However, the swiping motion also scrolls in the dropdown. Different functionalities that require the same input limits accessibility for users with impaired motor control.

II: Output

After exploring inputs, I also explored outputs from the dropdown menus on these three applications. The inputs can change the state of a dropdown component, and the output reflects these state changes to the user. So, I explored outputs including colors, text label, focus order, and screen reader output.

EdStem Web App Calendar Mac App/Mobile App Starbucks Web App/Mobile App
Colors On the EdStem website on Mac, the button to open the filter dropdown menu turns blue on hover. This output sends the signal to the user that this 'Filter' text is actually a clickable button. On both Mac and mobible, when clicked, the button becomes a deeper blue, signalling to the user that they have successfully clicked the button and can now choose an option. Similarly, when hovering over each of the dropdown options, they turn blue, signalling to the user again that they can click one of these options. Finally, once they select a filter from the dropdown, the filter button stays a deep blue, even after being de-selected, to represent that the selected filter has been applied. When I hover over the calendar dropdown menu in Apple Calendar, the color of the dropdown button does not change. Once I select the dropdown menu, the button goes out of sight on both Mac and mobile. However, one way that this component does use color is that each calendar is assigned a color, and the button's color changes to the color of the calendar selected on both Mac and mobile. This color output can help efficiency, because common users know what color calendar they are looking for without needing to read the text in the dropdown menu. Hovering over the milk dropdown button on the Starbucks website on my Mac, the color of the dropdown button does not change. However, when I click on the dropdown menu button, on Mac and mobile, the button turns green. While visually appealing, I don't find this color output particularly useful, since when you click the button, the dropdown menu also appears, so you already know you've clicked the button. I would find this green color more useful if it appeared on hover, to signal that your mouse is in the right place to open the menu.
Text Label The button to open the dropdown menu for filtering initially is labeled with the text 'Filter' and a downward arrow. However, after the user selects a filter from the dropdown, the text label changes from 'Filter' to the name of the filter applied, such as 'Unread', as in my screenshots. This is a valuable piece of output for the user, because it reinforces that they selected the filter that they meant to. On Mac, there is no text label on the calendar dropdown button, only the color of the calendar and the up and down arrows. On mobile, the button does have text, and it is the name of the calendar currently selected for the event. When the selected calendar changes, the button's text changes to reflect the new event selected. This text is a valuable piece of output, since it verifies to users that they selected the correct calendar. At the top of the dropdown menu button, in smaller font than the label in the middle of the button, is “Milk”, to signal to users that this dropdown selects their milk option. Before the user selects a milk, the default milk is 2%, so the button is initially labeled with “2% Milk”. When the user selects a different milk, the text label changes to display the name of the new milk. I find this output useful, but I disagree with having a default milk option. Instead, I think the selection should start as something like “Not Selected”, and the user shouldn't be able to buy the drink without selecting a milk. An error message output would prevent people from skipping over this dropdown and ending up with milk that they don't like or cannot drink.
Focus Order Using the tab key, I can focus on different components along the top of the EdStem website. Once I reach the filter button, I can use the return key to open the dropdown menu. Once I have done so, then I have to use the up and down arrows to switch between options, and the tab key does nothing. I don't think needing to switch from the tab key to the arrow keys would make sense to someone unable to see the screen. I think there is no problem with the up and down keys working here, but I think the tab key should continue to work even when the dropdown menu is open. As described in the inputs section, I can tab between calendar events, and then use the return key to open editing for a specific event. However, when I use the tab key inside this editing window, the dropdown menu is never focused on. Not being able to focus on the dropdown menu of course impedes the users providing inputs, but it also blocks the outputs that users can get from this application. If they cannot focus on the dropdown menu, then visually impaired users may not know it exists. In the input section above, I explained that I could focus on this dropdown using the tab key, but when I pressed enter, I was unable to open the dropdown menu. This prevents an important output signal, which is that the user has selected the right component to open the dropdown menu. Since the return key does nothing, the user might be confused and think that they have focused on the wrong component in their effort to customize their milk selection. In reality, the application has just mismatched user needs.
Screen Reader On my Mac, I used voice over to explore the different verbal descriptions from different states of the EdStem website. When I first navigate to the 'Filter'' button that opens the dropdown and focus on it, the text that VoiceOver reads out is "Filter, mean pop up collapsed, button, Thread list, region". This tells the user the name of the button, that the button brings up a menu that is currently collapsed, and that it is selectable. When I opened the dropdown menu, the text that VoiceOver read out was "menu, ..." followed by each of the dropdown options. However, as I used the up and down keys to move through options, VoiceOver did not read out their labels. It only read out these labels and called each a "menu item" when I hovered over the options with my mouse. Surprisingly, when I turned VoiceOver on, I was suddenly able to use the tab key to get to the calendar dropdown menu. I turned VoiceOver off again just to confirm that I still wasn't able to get there without it. I find it confusing that the tab key works differently when one screen reader, VoiceOver, is on. Not all visually impaired users may use VoiceOver as their screen reader, and they should still be able to use this button. Regardless, when I had the button focused with VoiceOver, it read “Calendar, popover, sarah_ridley@brown.edu, pop up button”, where “sarah_ridley@brown.edu” was the name of the current calendar selected. However, I don't think that “popover” is the clearest way to signal that this button opens a dropdown menu. Once the menu was open, it read out the names of each option, and then specified that each was a “menu item”. This makes sense to me, and I think it would be more useful as output if VoiceOver specified that the button opened this menu of items. Just as with Apple Calendar, turning on VoiceOver changed my keyboard interaction options. Now, when I focused on the milk dropdown button with VoiceOver on, it read “2% Milk, Milk, menu pop up collapsed, button”. Compared to the Apple Calendar VoiceOver output, I found the explanation that this was a collapsed pop up menu much clearer than just “popover”. Then, VoiceOver instructed me to press Control + Option + Space to open the menu. Since this allowed me to open the menu, I turned VoiceOver off, and tried this keystroke again with the button focused. This still did not work, so I still have not figured out how to open the dropdown menu with just my keyboard and without VoiceOver. While I think this poses an accessibility problem, I do feel that VoiceOver gave valuable outputs instructing me how to use the button and what it did, which is important for visually impaired users.

III: State Models & Component Redesign

After exploring input and output from EdStem, Apple Calendar, and Starbucks, I had several ideas about how to improve the dropdown components found in all three applications. However, this case study will focus on my redesign of the calendar dropdown menu in Apple Calendar.

Before beginning my redesign, I created state models to display the current interactions possible with Apple Calendar's calendar dropdown menu. State models show both the available states of the component, as well as the actions that transition between states. Below, the two state models displaying pre-revision interactions with the touchpad and with the keyboard respectively. Note that I often refer to pink highlights or borders, this is a Mac system setting that users customize.

Initial Calendar Dropdown Menu Component: Mouse User

Given that this is the state model for mouse interactions, the user's mouse position and clicks cause all transitions between states. Another important note is that "Open" means that the dropdown menu has appeared. As shown in the state model, the dropdown menu stays visible even when the mouse leaves the area, but disappears if the mouse clicks outside of the dropdown menu area.

Initial Calendar Dropdown Menu Component: Keyboard User

As described in the input and output sections, without VoiceOver, I was unable to focus on the dropdown menu button with only my keyboard on my Mac. Therefore, this state model is missing a transition from inactive to focus, although it should be noted that the button can be tabbed into focus when VoiceOver is active.

Once the calendar dropdown menu button is focused, then I am able to use the return key, up and down arrows, and escape key to transition between states, as shown.

To follow these initial state models, below I present two new state models for my revised calendar dropdown menu component, with mouse and with keyboard. The main design change I made includes adding a "Confirm" button to the dropdown meu. This choice will be further explored and justified in the Figma models presented below.

Revised Calendar Dropdown Menu Component: Mouse User

As mentioned, the main revision I made was introducing a "Confirm" button on the dropdown menu. So, after a user clicks one of the dropdown options, they also need to click the "Confirm" button to actually select their chosen option and close the dropdown menu. If they do not select "Confirm" after clicking an option, and instead click way, their selection will not be submitted.

Revised Calendar Dropdown Menu Component: Keyboard User

Most importantly, I added a transition from disabled to focused with the tab key, which the initial state model lacked.

Similar to the revised mouse version, the main change to this state model is introducing a "Confirm" button. Once the user has focused on an option with the up and down keys, when they press return, now their focused option is selected. This also automatically focuses on the "Confirm" button. Now, the user needs to press the return key again to activate the "Confirm" button and submit their selection.

Figma Models

The above Figma model is my redesign of the dropdown menu to assign an event to a particular calendar in Apple Calendar. This is the button with the blue square and the up/down arrows on the calendar event editing window. The model contains three frames, described below:

  • The first Figma frame displays the initial state of my revised calendar dropdown menu. This is the state before the user has interacted with the dropdown, so the button is visible but the dropdown menu window has not appeared.
  • When the user opens the dropdown menu and hovers over or focuses on one of the options, they will see the second frame. So, the dropdown menu is open and one of the options is highlighted. However, the user has not clicked on their selection yet. After clicking on their selection, they will still need to confirm it with the "Confirm" button, discussed in the revised state models.
  • This is a third, bonus state that I believe is important to understanding my redesign. After the user clicks on (or presses return on) an option, that option is not yet submitted. Instead, the user still must press the Confirm button in the dropdown menu to submit their selection and close the dropdown menu.

My revised component increases accessibility at the expense of efficiency. Adding a "Confirm" button means that frequent users have one more click or key to press before they can submit their selection from the dropdown menu. However, this increases accessibility beacuse it means that users with fine motor control issues will cannot accidentally submit their selection with one click of the mouse or key press. Adding an extra confirmation transition means that these users can accidentally select an option, but change their selection without the window closing (which is the currenet behavior of the component when any option is clicked).

This change also improves learnability and memorability in the sense that it lets users know that the things that have popped up on screen are all options to click, as implied by the "Confirm" button. They can play around with clicking these options without the window closing every time, and these lower stakes help users get comfortable with the component faster.

In other ways, my redesign increases accessibility, without risking other properties. For example, since I found that I could not focus on the calendar dropdown menu outside of VoiceOver, in my redesign, I specified that the tab key could be used to focus on the button. This does not reduce learnability, memorability, nor efficiency, it only increases accessibility.

IV: Reflection

When exploring the usability of the dropdown menus in EdStem, Apple Calendar, and Starbucks, all three applications had interesting uses of colors to reflect state back to the user. This guided me to use colors that send signals to users about the state of the component in my redesign, specifically designing how the hovered option is highlighted in different colors than the selected option.

Comparing the accessibility of these three applications, the most accessible component was EdStem's dropdown menu, since it was the only one that I could fully navigate with the keyboard without turning on VoiceOver. So, keyboard navigability was an acessibility consideration missing from Apple Calendar and Starbucks, and in my revised Apple Calendar dropdown state model, I specified that the dropdown could be opened with the keyboard.

When thinking about making components more accessible, it's useful to think through the lens of mismatches, or situations where an interface creates unnecessary barriers for certain users due to a lack of inclusive thinking. In my redesign, adding keyboard navigability addresses the mismatch where users that only use the keyboard can still interact with this component. Similarly, adding the "Confirm" button addresses the mismatch where some users may accidentally press or interact with these components, and the confirmation step is more forgiving of accidental clicks.

In this sense, the lack of keyboard accessibility on the Apple Calendar and Starbucks dropdowns negatively impacting those with visual impairments, since focusing on the dropdown was impossible without VoiceOver, which not all impaired users might use. On the other hand, ARIA text read by VoiceOver is very beneficial to those with visual impairments, and I found that of the three applications, Starbucks' components had the most clear and descriptive ARIA text.

Thinking more generally, it seems that the mouse is the most often prioritized when designing components, as many designers are fully abled, but this means that they may forget to consider and design for the experiences of those who are impaired.
